ABSTRACT

Objective: This study aimed to examine the prevalence of dyslipidemia, evaluate
management patterns, and explore related health behaviors among South Korean adults
through an analysis of the 2024 Dyslipidemia Fact Sheet.
Methods: This study utilized data from the Korea National Health and Nutrition Examination
Survey (2007?2022) and the National Health Insurance Service-National Sample Cohort
(2002?2019) to assess lipid profiles, trends in prevalence, management indicators, and health
behaviors in South Koreans aged 20 and over. The 2005 Korean demographic distribution
was applied as the reference for age standardization.
Results: The crude prevalence of dyslipidemia during 2016?2022 was 40.9% under
standard criteria, increasing to 47.4% when modified high-density lipoprotein-cholesterol
criteria for women were applied. From 2007 to 2022, the age-adjusted prevalence of
hypercholesterolemia rose markedly from 8.8% to 22.4%, while the crude prevalence
reached 27.4%. Indicators of hypercholesterolemia management showed improvement,
with awareness, treatment, and control rates of 68.0%, 61.2%, and 54.1%, respectively.
Comorbidity analysis demonstrated a markedly higher prevalence of dyslipidemia in
adults with diabetes (87.0%), hypertension (72.4%), and obesity (55.2%). Health behavior
evaluation revealed suboptimal adherence to dietary and physical activity recommendations.
Conclusion: Despite advances in detection and treatment, dyslipidemia remains highly
prevalent in South Korea, particularly among individuals with metabolic comorbidities.
Given the insufficient adherence to dietary and physical activity guidelines, strengthened
strategies for prevention and management are warranted, with an emphasis on lifestyle
modification and aggressive treatment approaches in high-risk groups.

INTRODUCTION

Dyslipidemia is defined as abnormal lipid patterns in the blood, characterized by one or
more of the following: elevated low-density lipoprotein cholesterol (LDL-C), increased
triglycerides (TGs), reduced high-density lipoprotein cholesterol (HDL-C), or combinations
of these irregularities.1 It remains a major modifiable risk factor for cardiovascular disease,
which continues to be one of the leading causes of death worldwide.1,2 The World Health
Organization estimated that in 2008 approximately 39% of adults aged 25 years and older had
hypercholesterolemia, with elevated LDL-C contributing to more than one-third of deaths
from ischemic heart disease and stroke.3,4 These findings highlight the critical need for
improved strategies in cholesterol management and highlight the importance of accurately
understanding prevalence, patterns, and trends within specific populations.

Since 2015, the Korean Society of Lipid and Atherosclerosis has published
the Dyslipidemia Fact Sheet (DFS) to systematically report on the status of
dyslipidemia in South Korea, covering prevalence, awareness, treatment, control,
and related lifestyle behaviors.2 The most recent data indicate that the prevalence of
hypercholesterolemia increased more than 2.5-fold between 2007 and 2022, reinforcing
the need for ongoing surveillance and targeted interventions.2 To address this concern,
the present study analyzed recent data from the Korea National Health and Nutrition
Examination Survey (KNHANES, 2007?2022) and the National Health Insurance Service-
National Sample Cohort (NHIS-NSC, 2002?2019) to provide an updated overview of lipid
profiles, prevalence trends, management outcomes, and health behaviors in Korean adults
aged 20 years and older. By evaluating temporal changes, this study offers insight into
the evolving epidemiology of dyslipidemia in South Korea and supports the development of
future public health strategies.

MATERIALS AND METHODS

1. Data source and study participants
This study analyzed KNHANES datasets spanning 2007?2022, complemented by NHIS-NSC
data from 2002?2019.5,6 The KNHANES is a nationwide survey that evaluates health status
and nutritional patterns in the Korean population. It tracks temporal changes in major health
risk factors and monitors the prevalence and management of chronic diseases. The survey
uses a complex, stratified sampling method with weighting to represent the demographic
distribution of community-dwelling Koreans aged ¡Ã1 year across the country. For this
study, participants aged 20 years or older with available fasting lipid measurements and
self-reported physician-diagnosed dyslipidemia, including treatment status, were included.
The anonymized datasets are publicly available through the Korea Centers for Disease
Control and Prevention website.

This study was conducted in accordance with the principles outlined in the Declaration of
Helsinki (2024), and all procedures adhered to relevant guidelines and regulations. Because
KNHANES data are fully anonymized, the study was approved by the Institutional Review
Board (IRB) of The Catholic University of Medicine, Eunpyeong St. Mary¡¯s Hospital, which
waived the requirement for informed consent (IRB No. PIRB-20240711-001). The manuscript
was prepared in compliance with the Strengthening the Reporting of Observational Studies
in Epidemiology guidelines to ensure completeness and transparency.

2. Measurements and definitions
Fasting serum lipid profiles?including total cholesterol, TG, LDL-C, and HDL-C?
were obtained after at least 8 hours of fasting. When LDL-C values were not directly
measured, they were calculated using the Friedewald formula. Non-HDL cholesterol
was calculated by subtracting HDL-C from total cholesterol. Hypercholesterolemia was
defined as total cholesterol ¡Ã240 mg/dL or current use of lipid-lowering medication.7-9
Awareness was defined as the proportion of individuals who reported being diagnosed with
hypercholesterolemia by a physician. The treatment rate was defined as the proportion using
lipid-lowering medication. Control rates were evaluated in 2 ways: the proportion of all
hypercholesterolemia patients with total cholesterol <200 mg/dL, and the proportion within
the subgroup receiving treatment.

Dyslipidemia was classified into 3 categories: hyper-LDL-cholesterolemia,
hypertriglyceridemia, and hypo-HDL-cholesterolemia. Hyper-LDL-cholesterolemia was
defined as LDL-C ¡Ã160 mg/dL or use of lipid-lowering medication. Hypertriglyceridemia was
defined as TG ¡Ã200 mg/dL. Hypo-HDL-cholesterolemia was defined in 2 ways: type
1, HDL-C <40 mg/dL in both sexes; and type 2, HDL-C <40 mg/dL in men or <50 mg/
dL in women. Accordingly, Dyslipidemia 1 was defined as hyper-LDL-cholesterolemia,
hypertriglyceridemia, or hypo-HDL-cholesterolemia using the type 1 definition;
Dyslipidemia 2 was defined similarly but with the type 2 definition.

3. Specific populations and health behaviors
Data from 2016 to 2022 were analyzed to evaluate dyslipidemia in individuals with chronic
conditions. Diabetes was defined as fasting glucose ¡Ã126 mg/dL, glycated hemoglobin
¡Ã6.5%, prior physician diagnosis, or current use of glucose-lowering medication.
Hypertension was defined as systolic blood pressure ¡Ã140 mmHg, diastolic blood pressure
¡Ã90 mmHg, or current use of antihypertensive drugs. Obesity was defined as body mass
index (BMI) ¡Ã25 kg/m2, while abdominal obesity was defined as a waist circumference greater
than 90 cm for men and 85 cm for women.10 Health behaviors were assessed using KNHANES
2016?2018 data. Dietary intake was evaluated by single-day dietary recall and adherence to
recommended macronutrient distributions. Physical activity was measured using the Global
Physical Activity Questionnaire, and participants were classified as physically active or
inactive based on weekly activity duration.11 Smoking and alcohol use were also analyzed.
Regular alcohol use was defined as drinking more than once per month in the past year.
Current smoking was defined as smoking at least 100 cigarettes in a lifetime and currently
smoking, while ex-smoking was defined as having smoked at least 100 cigarettes in a lifetime
but not smoking in the past 28 days.12

4. Statistical analysis
All statistical analyses incorporated sampling weights for the multistage probability design
to ensure national representativeness. Both crude and age-standardized estimates of
dyslipidemia prevalence and management indicators were calculated. Age-standardized
prevalence was based on the projected 2005 Korean population stratified by age and sex.
Weighted proportions were used to examine differences in prevalence and management
by demographic characteristics and health behaviors. Trend analyses from 2007 to 2022
assessed changes in dyslipidemia prevalence, awareness, treatment, and control. Sex-
specific differences in lipid profiles were tested using the independent samples t-test,
while prevalence differences were evaluated with the Rao?Scott chi-square test. Statistical
significance was set at p<0.05 (2-tailed). Additional analyses examined dyslipidemia patterns

RESULTS

1. Lipid profile in Korean adults
From 2007 to 2022, temporal analysis of age-standardized lipid parameters, referenced
to the 2005 Korean demographic structure, demonstrated distinct trends (Fig. 1A). Total
cholesterol levels increased steadily from 186.2 mg/dL in 2007 to 192.7 mg/dL in 2022. During
the same period, HDL-C showed the greatest improvement, rising from 48.1 mg/dL to 57.7
mg/dL. Non-HDL cholesterol fluctuated but ultimately declined slightly from 138.1 mg/dL
to 135.0 mg/dL, while LDL-C rose gradually from 112.1 mg/dL to 118.5 mg/dL, showing an
upward trend regardless of whether measured directly or calculated (Supplementary Table 1).
TG levels remained relatively stable, shifting only slightly from 130.2 mg/dL to 129.3 mg/dL.
Sex-specific analysis revealed marked differences in lipid profiles (Fig. 1B). Men consistently
had higher non-HDL cholesterol concentrations (139¡¾40 mg/dL vs. 135¡¾38 mg/dL) and TG
(151¡¾124 mg/dL vs. 112¡¾73 mg/dL) compared with women (both p<0.001). In contrast, women
maintained higher total cholesterol levels (192¡¾39 mg/dL vs. 188¡¾40 mg/dL in men), LDL-C
levels (115¡¾35 mg/dL vs. 113¡¾35 mg/dL in men), and HDL-C levels (57¡¾14 mg/dL vs. 49¡¾12 mg/
dL in men (all p<0.001), indicating significant sex-based differences in lipid metabolism.

2. Dyslipidemia
Analysis of dyslipidemia prevalence showed divergent patterns depending on classification
criteria and demographic variables. In 2022, the age-adjusted prevalence was 37.5% for
Dyslipidemia 1 and 41.4% for Dyslipidemia 2 (Fig. 2A). The mean crude prevalence from
2016?2022 was notably higher, at 40.9% and 47.4%, respectively (Fig. 2B).

Sex-based analysis revealed a higher prevalence of Dyslipidemia 1 among men (47.1%) than
among women (34.7%) (p<0.001), whereas Dyslipidemia 2 was comparable between men
(47.1%) and women (47.7%) (p=0.47). The sex-related difference was more pronounced in
older populations, with the highest prevalence of Dyslipidemia 2 observed among women in
their 70s (74.4%), compared to 51.4% in men of the same age group (p<0.001).

Hyper-LDL-cholesterolemia
From 2007 to 2022, the age-adjusted prevalence of hyper-LDL-cholesterolemia rose
markedly, increasing from 8.8% to 23.4% (Supplementary Fig. 1A). The crude prevalence
during 2016?2022 was 24.2%, with notable sex differences (Supplementary Fig. 1B). Rates
were highest in individuals in their 60s, reaching 33.2% in men and 50.0% in women.

Hypertriglyceridemia
Hypertriglyceridemia prevalence remained relatively stable, with age-standardized rates
decreasing slightly from 15.3% in 2007 to 14.1% in 2022 (Supplementary Fig. 2A). Sex
differences were most evident in middle age, particularly in the 40s, where men had
significantly higher prevalence than women (28.9% vs. 7.2%) (Supplementary Fig. 2B).

3. Hypercholesterolemia
The prevalence of hypercholesterolemia increased substantially from 2007 to 2022. Age-
standardized prevalence rose from 8.8% to 22.4%, representing more than a twofold increase
(Fig. 3A). Crude prevalence rose even more sharply, reaching 27.4% in 2022, indicating that
nearly 1 in 4 Korean adults has hypercholesterolemia (Fig. 3B). Management indicators
showed substantial improvement over time. By 2022, the awareness rate reached 68.0%, with
61.2% of affected individuals receiving treatment. The overall control rate was 54.1%, while
among those on lipid-lowering medications, 87.4% achieved target cholesterol levels (Fig. 4).

4. Dyslipidemia in specific populations
Analysis of dyslipidemia prevalence across different metabolic conditions revealed significant
variations. Among individuals with diabetes, prevalence increased stepwise with worsening
glucose metabolism (Fig. 5A). Rates rose from 25.2% in normoglycemic individuals to
50.7% in those with prediabetes and 70.2% in individuals with diabetes. When applying
the stricter LDL-C threshold of ¡Ã100 mg/dL for diabetes, prevalence rose markedly to 87.0%.
Similar trends were observed with blood pressure status (Fig. 5B). Dyslipidemia prevalence
increased from 27.6% in normotensive individuals to 42.6% in prehypertension and 61.2%
in hypertension. Target achievement rates varied by clinical definition. Among individuals
with diabetes, 56.3% met the LDL-C target of <100 mg/dL (Supplementary Fig. 5A), but
only 24.6% reached the more stringent target of <70 mg/dL. In those with hypertension,
74.4% achieved the goal when the criterion was LDL-C <130 mg/dL (Supplementary Fig. 5B),
whereas only 44.9% achieved target levels when the cutoff was set at <100 mg/dL.

Body composition also showed a strong association with dyslipidemia prevalence (Fig. 5C).
Prevalence followed a clear gradient across BMI categories: 11.7% in underweight, 27.4% in
normal weight, 44.2% in overweight, and 55.2% in obese individuals. Abdominal obesity
demonstrated an even stronger effect, with prevalence nearly doubling from 30.8% in
individuals without central obesity to 59.0% in those with abdominal obesity (Fig. 5D).

5. Health behaviors
The analysis of dietary patterns among adults with dyslipidemia revealed varying levels of
adherence to nutritional recommendations (Fig. 6A). While most participants consumed
protein within recommended levels (82.5% of men, 86.7% of women), compliance with
carbohydrate guidelines was low (33.6% of men, 31.0% of women). Approximately half of
adults achieved the recommended total energy intake (50.6% of men, 47.4% of women),
while fat intake showed moderate adherence (58.0% of men, 55.2% of women). Vegetable
consumption was particularly inadequate, with only 31.1% of men and 27.2% of women
meeting the recommended daily intake (Fig. 6B). Despite some improvement over time,

DISCUSSION

Journal of

Lipid and

Atherosclerosis

The prevalence of dyslipidemia among adults in Korea has shown significant changes since
the initial DFS publications. Previous editions documented crude prevalence rates of 47.8%
(2015 DFS), 40.5% (2018 DFS), 38.4% (2020 DFS), and 48% (2022 DFS). The current 2024 DFS
demonstrates a renewed increase, with prevalence reaching 47.4% in the general population
(47.1% in men and 47.7% in women) under the Dyslipidemia 2 definition. This trend parallels
rising rates of hypercholesterolemia, which affected 27.4% of the population in 2022.

Total cholesterol and LDL-C levels steadily increased, while HDL-C improved in a favorable
direction. Non-HDL cholesterol fluctuated but slightly decreased overall, and TG levels have
remained relatively stable. Although TG and HDL-C are generally inversely related,13 this
association can be influenced by multiple factors, including body weight, dietary patterns,
alcohol consumption, and physical activity,14 and thus may not always align. The observed
rise in HDL-C despite stable TG levels, coupled with increasing LDL-C, likely reflects
multifactorial determinants beyond direct TG metabolism.15 These findings suggest a mixed
impact of public health interventions and lifestyle changes. From an atherogenic risk
standpoint, the continued increase in total cholesterol and LDL-C is concerning, although
the modest decline in non-HDL cholesterol may partially mitigate risk.16 These findings
underscore that stable TG levels should not be misinterpreted as evidence of an overall
improvement in lipid-associated cardiovascular risk.

Although management metrics have improved, with awareness and treatment rates of
hypercholesterolemia increasing and the control rate rising substantially from 19.1%
to 54.1% over the past decade, nearly half of affected individuals remain inadequately
controlled. Interpretation of these rates requires caution, as current guidelines emphasize
LDL-C as the primary therapeutic target.1,17 Despite advances in awareness, treatment, and
control, mean total cholesterol and LDL-C levels in the overall population have continued
to climb. Similar paradoxical findings have been reported in other national surveys.18-20
One likely explanation is that management indicators are calculated among diagnosed
and treated individuals, whereas population averages include newly developed, untreated
cases. Persistent westernized dietary habits, characterized by high intake of saturated
fats and sugar-sweetened beverages and low intake of vegetables and dietary fiber21,22
along with sedentary lifestyles,23 likely contribute to the influx of new dyslipidemia cases,
counteracting therapeutic gains. Our data further confirm suboptimal adherence to dietary
and physical activity recommendations among individuals with dyslipidemia. In addition,
treatment intensity in primary care may remain insufficient, with frequent reliance on
low-dose statins and delayed escalation of therapy.24 Younger individuals, who often remain
asymptomatic, are also more likely to be under-screened, undertreated, or less adherent to
therapy.25,26 Meanwhile, Korea¡¯s universal healthcare system, which facilitates broad access
to medical care including lipid-lowering therapy, in conjunction with ongoing education
for both patients and physicians, may have contributed to improvements in awareness and
treatment rates. However, these gains have not yet reversed the rising trend in population-
level cholesterol, indicating that more comprehensive strategies are required. Strengthening
hypercholesterolemia management will require not only intensified pharmacologic treatment
but also robust, population-wide lifestyle interventions to address dietary westernization and
physical inactivity. Expanding targeted screening to younger adults and reinforcing LDL-C?
specific treatment goals as quality indicators in clinical practice may further enhance long-
term cardiovascular risk reduction.

Sex-specific analysis revealed distinct patterns. Under the Dyslipidemia 2 definition,
prevalence in men remained relatively stable through middle age before declining
slightly after 60, while prevalence in women continued to rise, peaking at 74.4% in
their 70s. This pattern in women is largely attributable to elevated rates of hyper-LDL-
cholesterolemia (46.9%) and hypo-HDL-cholesterolemia (44.1%) in older age groups,
reflecting postmenopausal metabolic changes in cholesterol regulation.27 Although overall
awareness and treatment rates for hypercholesterolemia were comparable between men
and women, the age-related deterioration in women¡¯s lipid profiles highlights the need
for targeted interventions in postmenopausal women. The control rates among treated
individuals were similar between sexes, indicating equitable therapeutic efficacy once
treatment is initiated.

The strong associations between dyslipidemia and metabolic disorders, including diabetes,
hypertension, and obesity, underscore the interconnected nature of these cardiometabolic
risk factors. Dyslipidemia prevalence reaches 87.0% in adults with diabetes and 72.4% in
those with hypertension when adjusted LDL-C thresholds are applied. The stepwise increase
in prevalence with worsening metabolic profiles highlights the importance of integrated
management strategies that address multiple risk factors simultaneously. Furthermore,
only 56.3% of patients with diabetes achieved the recommended LDL-C target of <100 mg/
dL, while 74.4% of individuals with hypertension met their LDL-C target of <130 mg/dL.
These findings emphasize the critical need for stricter LDL-C control in high-risk groups to
effectively reduce cardiovascular risk.28

This study has several inherent limitations. First, its cross-sectional design restricts
the ability to infer causality between observed patterns and their underlying determinants.
Second, reliance on self-reported data regarding health behaviors and physician-diagnosed
hypercholesterolemia may introduce recall or reporting bias. Third, it was not possible to
directly compare health behaviors?including diet, smoking, alcohol consumption, and
physical activity?between individuals with and without dyslipidemia. Fourth, although this
study evaluated awareness, treatment, and control rates for hypercholesterolemia, current
guidelines emphasize LDL-C target?based management.1,17 Comprehensive risk stratification
of the entire population with corresponding LDL-C control rates could not be performed
within the framework of this nationwide survey. Instead, LDL-C control rates were assessed
only in populations with common comorbidities, such as diabetes mellitus and hypertension.
Finally, no information was available on medication therapy, which may influence lipid
profiles. Further analyses of treatment patterns and temporal changes in pharmacotherapy
are warranted.

In conclusion, the 2024 DFS highlights the persistent and increasing burden of
dyslipidemia in South Korea, including rising rates of hypercholesterolemia. Although
awareness and treatment indicators have improved, control rates remain inadequate,
particularly among high-risk populations. Unhealthy behaviors?including insufficient
physical activity, poor dietary habits, smoking, and alcohol consumption?likely exacerbate
the problem. These findings underscore the need for comprehensive management strategies
and targeted interventions for individuals with comorbid conditions. Future efforts should
prioritize integrated approaches that combine lifestyle modification with aggressive
treatment protocols to mitigate this major public health challenge.
